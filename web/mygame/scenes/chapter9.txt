You walk.

The mud under your boots is stiffening, fighting you with every step. 
It turns from sludge to iron as the temperature drops, locking the earth into a grey, unyielding grimace.

Ashwood feels [i]hollowed out[/i]. Scraped clean. Like a fruit left to rot on the vine, the skin intact but the sweetness inside turned to black, buzzing pulp.

Usually, this time of day is loud. It should be a living thing. 
The rhythmic clang-clang-clang of the smithy’s hammer singing against hot steel. The haggle of fishmongers arguing over the price of river trout. 
The shrieking of children chasing stray dogs through the puddles, their laughter cutting through the gloom.

But today, the anvil is cold. The trout lie dead and staring in unsalted barrels. The children are hidden behind bolted doors.

[i]Today, there is only the wind.[/i]

It drags its belly through the eaves, whistling low and sharp through the cracks in the timber. It hunts through the empty alleyways, searching for something it can’t find.

It smells of wet wool. Of stagnant water. And smoke.

Old smoke. The kind that doesn’t wash out. The kind that clings to the thatch and the stone and the hair on your arms. It sits heavy in the air, greasy and distinct.

You taste it on the back of your tongue. Acrid. [i]Bitter.[/i]

You pass the baker’s shop. The door is barred.

No one looks at you.

A few shapes move in the periphery—shadows flitting between alleyways, heads bowed, shoulders hunched.
